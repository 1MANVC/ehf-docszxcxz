Design Principles — What We Must Get Right
Temporal Discipline
Analysis uses ONLY information available before a price move. LLMs naturally explain outcomes — every layer is designed to prevent hindsight contamination.
Information Isolation
Bull and bear perspectives are constructed independently. No hedging, no "on the other hand." Each must commit to its strongest argument.
AI Judges, Code Computes
LLMs apply analytical judgment — which metric matters and why. All arithmetic (valuations, ratios, implied expectations) is deterministic code. Eliminates an entire class of errors.
Non-Determinism Is Managed
AI models can produce different outputs from identical inputs. Validation gates, schema enforcement, and structured outputs catch quality variance before it reaches the memo.
Wrong-but-Explicit > Vague-but-Safe
A specific call that can be proven wrong is more valuable than a hedge that can never be falsified. The system takes positions and states assumptions clearly.
Investment Process Drives Architecture
Every design decision starts with "what would a good analyst do?" The technology serves the investment process, not the other way around.
Why This Is Hard — The Demo-to-Production Gap
Challenge 01
LLMs Are Trained on Hindsight
Naive approach: "Only use information before date X"
You can't prompt your way out of this. LLMs leak future knowledge through framing, emphasis, and word choice even when told not to. The system must structurally prevent contamination — controlling what data the model can see, not just asking it to ignore what it knows.
Challenge 02
AI Wants to Hedge, Not Commit
Naive approach: "Give me a strong bull case"
RLHF literally trains models to be balanced and non-committal. Getting an AI to build a genuinely independent thesis with specific assumptions and kill criteria — not "growth is strong but valuation is stretched" — requires architectural separation, not just prompting. Each perspective must be constructed without seeing the other.
Challenge 03
Same Input, Different Output
Naive approach: "Test it once, ship it"
Run the same ticker twice, get materially different quality. Non-determinism isn't a bug — it's inherent to the technology. You need validation gates that catch variance, schema enforcement that prevents structural drift, and regression testing across runs. Most AI demos quietly cherry-pick the good outputs.
Challenge 04
LLMs Can't Do Math
Naive approach: "Calculate the implied P/E ratio"
An LLM will confidently give you a wrong number. Every numerical claim must be computed deterministically by code — valuations, growth rates, implied expectations. The AI's job is to decide which metric matters and why. Python does the arithmetic. Mixing these two roles is how you get plausible-looking memos with fabricated numbers.
Challenge 05
Industry Knowledge Without Industry Bias
Naive approach: "Here's everything about the solar industry"
Vault knowledge must teach the model HOW to think about a sector without telling it WHAT to conclude. Feed current-state industry data directly and the model anchors on it instead of reasoning independently. The boundary between "analytical framework" and "conclusion bias" is subtle and breaks easily — especially across vaults where cross-sector signals can amplify bias.
Challenge 06
Compound Failures Are Silent
Naive approach: "Each component works, so the system works"
Individual components can be correct but their interaction produces the wrong answer. A consensus data module works. A cyclical trough detector works. Together, they can over-anchor a valuation and flip a recommendation. You have to test the system as a whole, not just the parts — and the failure modes are different every time.
"Anyone can get an AI to write something that looks like an investment memo.
The hard part is making it produce something you'd actually trust enough to trade on.
That's the difference between a demo and a system."
